\begin{abstract}
This report examines whether ThuggerDaily's X/Twitter posting activity was temporally associated with measurable shifts in public discourse around the Young Thug / YSL RICO trial. The analysis uses a unified processed corpus of 165,565 public records from 8 platforms, including 773 ThuggerDaily records, spanning 2022-05-01 through 2024-12-29. The central object is not legal truth, guilt, innocence, or judicial causation. It is public narrative response: volume, sentiment, engagement, topic prevalence, and the timing of cross-platform attention. The empirical strategy combines event-window analysis, lag correlation, topic-shift analysis, and a ThuggerDaily-aligned exposure signal that compares post-event public outcomes on days with and without observed ThuggerDaily activity. Results support the presence of event-linked discourse shifts and platform-level attention patterns, but they do not prove randomized causal effects. The most defensible interpretation is that ThuggerDaily supplied a visible, recurring amplification channel whose activity sometimes coincided with downstream movement in public attention and sentiment, especially around high-salience legal moments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
High-profile criminal trials generate a public narrative ecosystem: courtroom proceedings, institutional reporting, platform-level conversation, and rapid recirculation by influential accounts. NarrativePulse is a measurement system for that ecosystem. This report uses the YSL RICO trial as a case study and asks a focused question: when the ThuggerDaily account posts about the trial, do we see time-linked shifts in the volume, sentiment, engagement, and topic mix of public discourse across platforms?

Two boundaries are important. First, this report does not evaluate legal truth, guilt, innocence, or judicial decision-making. Second, because the data are observational, it does not claim randomized causal effects. The appropriate language is \emph{temporal association}, \emph{event-linked discourse shift}, and \emph{influence signal}---not courtroom causation.

\section{Trial Background and Timeline}
The YSL RICO case spans multiple years and includes distinct phases that matter for how the public talks about it. Early periods are dominated by the shock of the indictment and bond decisions; the trial phase introduces repeated procedural moments; later periods include disruptions (such as recusal events) and resolution moments (including plea outcomes and the close of proceedings for remaining defendants).

For the purposes of narrative measurement, the key concept is that \textbf{trial events are attention shocks}. They change what information is available, what media can credibly report, and what the public is reacting to. In parallel, \textbf{music releases and artist news are competing shocks}: they can redirect conversation away from strictly legal framing and toward identity, fandom, loyalty, and career trajectories. ThuggerDaily sits inside this environment as a fast, legible amplification node.

\section{Executive Summary}
NarrativePulse was built to convert a broad public-data project into a professional legal-media intelligence dashboard. The motivating question is whether a single high-visibility X/Twitter account, ThuggerDaily, appeared to influence public discourse surrounding the YSL RICO trial. The account is treated as an observable exposure source. Trial events are treated as shocks to public attention. Downstream public reaction is measured across YouTube, X/Twitter, magazines, local news, newspapers, Spotify, Billboard, and Instagram.

The processed dashboard corpus contains \textbf{165,565} records, \textbf{773} of which are ThuggerDaily posts. Entity coverage is distributed across Young Thug (124,733 records), Gunna (38,068 records), and YFN Lucci (2,764 records). The largest source in the unified corpus is YouTube, followed by X/Twitter and media sources. This platform imbalance matters: YouTube comments and video statistics dominate the count distribution, while ThuggerDaily itself is concentrated on X/Twitter.

The report's core finding is careful but meaningful: public discourse did shift around major trial dates, and ThuggerDaily activity often appeared near the same windows. However, the strongest valid claim is an \emph{public narrative influence signal}, not proof of causal responsibility. Around the Young Thug plea and release event on October 31, 2024, the seven-day event window showed a change in observed public attention and sentiment, while ThuggerDaily posted in the same post-event period. Around procedural shocks such as Judge Ural Glanville's recusal, public discourse also moved, but the magnitude of movement that is directionally aligned with ThuggerDaily exposure days depends strongly on platform coverage and denominator assumptions.

This distinction is essential. Trial outcomes are produced by legal institutions, filings, courtroom decisions, counsel strategy, plea negotiations, and judicial process. Social media can shape public narrative, amplify interpretations, and change what audiences notice, but this dataset cannot identify a randomized causal pathway from tweets to court outcomes. The phrase ``affected the trial'' is therefore interpreted here as: \emph{affected the public narrative around the trial, and potentially the public-facing information environment in which the trial was discussed.}

\section{Research Question and Product Framing}
\subsection{Primary Question}
How did ThuggerDaily's X/Twitter posts temporally align with public sentiment, engagement, topic prevalence, and discourse volume around Young Thug, Gunna, and YFN Lucci across platforms?

\subsection{Interpretive Boundary}
The analysis avoids the claim that ThuggerDaily caused legal outcomes. Instead, it estimates:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item temporal association between ThuggerDaily posts and downstream public discourse;
\item event-linked discourse shifts around trial dates;
\item platform-level public reaction after salient legal events;
\item topic and sentiment movement in windows surrounding observed exposure.
\end{itemize}

The central inferential challenge is confounding. Major courtroom events and ThuggerDaily posts often occur in the same period because both respond to the same underlying news. A spike in public discourse after a plea, for example, may be caused by the plea itself, by mainstream media coverage, by courtroom livestream clips, by ThuggerDaily, by fan communities, or by all of these forces interacting. The report therefore treats ThuggerDaily as one visible node in a broader public attention system.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Unified Record Construction}
The ingestion script reads cleaned source exports from social media, video, news, music, and chart folders. Because the original files have source-specific schemas, the adapter detects likely date, text, engagement, URL, author, and keyword fields. Entity assignment is based on explicit references to Young Thug, Gunna, YFN Lucci, or close aliases in text and keyword fields. This method is transparent and reproducible, but it is not perfect. It can over-assign YSL-related records to Young Thug when a source talks broadly about the case.

\subsection{Sentiment}
Sentiment is scored as a lightweight public-language signal. The score is not treated as a psychological measure. Slang, sarcasm, legal vocabulary, fan language, and hip-hop discourse can all create misclassification. The report therefore emphasizes changes across time and groups rather than the absolute meaning of any individual score.

In the baseline pipeline, each record with text is assigned a continuous polarity score using a lexicon-based sentiment function (TextBlob polarity) applied to cleaned text. Polarity is measured on a \([-1, 1]\) scale, where negative values indicate more negative language and positive values indicate more positive language. For interpretability, the report also uses a coarse label mapping: \textbf{positive} when polarity \(> 0.1\), \textbf{negative} when polarity \(< -0.1\), and \textbf{neutral} otherwise. No domain-specific hip-hop slang dictionary or legal lexicon was applied in this baseline; instead, robustness is obtained by focusing on aggregates, platform stratification, and event-window contrasts. This makes sentiment useful as a directional signal, while keeping uncertainty explicit.

\subsection{Engagement}
Engagement is defined as:
\[
\text{Engagement Rate}
=
\frac{\text{Likes} + \text{Retweets} + \text{Comments}}
{\text{Exposure Denominator}}
\times 100.
\]
The exposure denominator varies by platform and may refer to impressions, views, analytics, or another available exposure metric. When no denominator is available, the report preserves raw engagement counts. For this reason, cross-platform engagement-rate comparisons should be interpreted as approximate and platform-conditioned.
When this report is paired with the Streamlit dashboard, denominator differences are explicitly flagged: the dashboard surfaces which platforms have usable exposure denominators and which views rely on raw engagement counts.

\subsection{Topic Leveling}
The project uses seven generalized topics: Live Music in Atlanta, Slang, Trial-related Content, Legal System and Judicial Process, Music, Social Media Slang and Emojis, and Free Thug Support. These are mapped into broader interpretive levels: legal, music, fandom/support, slang/social media, media/news, and local Atlanta culture.

\subsection{Event-Window Design}
For each key date, the notebook estimates seven-day pre/post changes:
\[
\Delta_y = \bar{Y}_{post} - \bar{Y}_{pre}.
\]
Outcomes include public volume, sentiment, engagement, and topic shares. The event-window output is descriptive and observational.

\subsection{ThuggerDaily-Aligned Exposure Signal}
The exposure-alignment signal asks: within the post-event window, are public outcomes different on days when ThuggerDaily posted compared with days when it did not? Formally, for an outcome \(Y\):
\[
\text{Exposure Lift} = \bar{Y}_{post, TD>0} - \bar{Y}_{post, TD=0}.
\]
The aligned share compares this lift to the observed event-window shift:
\[
\text{Aligned Share} = \frac{\text{Exposure Lift}}{\Delta_y}.
\]
This quantity is not causal responsibility. It is a timing-based public-discourse association signal. It is best read as: ``how much of the observed movement is directionally aligned with ThuggerDaily exposure days?''

\section{ThuggerDaily-Aligned Exposure Signal}
The exposure-alignment estimate is intentionally conservative. It does not say that ThuggerDaily ``caused'' a certain percent of the public shift. It says that the observed post-event shift can be compared with public outcomes on days where ThuggerDaily posted. If those days show higher public volume, stronger engagement, or different sentiment than non-exposure days, the report labels the result an aligned exposure signal.

\centering

The top ThuggerDaily-exposed event window in the Young Thug summary was \textbf{Young Thug pleads guilty and is released on probation terms}, with 27 ThuggerDaily posts and 1,005,245 observed ThuggerDaily engagement in the post window. The Young Thug plea event showed a sentiment change of 0.051 and a volume change of 3896 records in the seven-day design, with 27 ThuggerDaily post-window posts. The Glanville recusal event showed a sentiment change of 0.057, while opening statements showed a sentiment change of -0.014.

These figures are meaningful because they show when the account's activity was present in the same short-run public attention window. They are limited because ThuggerDaily is not the only exposure. Courtroom livestreams, Law\&Crime clips, mainstream articles, Reddit discussions, Instagram posts, and artist/music events all contribute to the public information environment.

\section{Lag Correlation}
Lag correlations test whether ThuggerDaily activity predicts downstream public metrics after 0, 1, 3, 7, 14, or 30 days. Correlation is not causation, but it is useful for determining whether the relationship has plausible temporal ordering. If same-day correlation is high but longer lags are weak, the account may be reacting to the same event as everyone else. If post-day lags remain elevated, that suggests possible persistence or delayed amplification.

In the seven-day event window around the October 31, 2024 plea/release, the share of \emph{Music} language increases while the share of \emph{Trial-related Content} and \emph{Legal System and Judicial Process} decreases. This does not mean the legal narrative disappears. It means that as uncertainty resolves, conversation partially rebalances toward music identity, fandom framing, and broader cultural talk.

Topic shifts are especially important for legal-media intelligence because sentiment alone can hide the substantive change. A neutral sentiment score attached to legal-process language may still represent a major public-narrative shift if the conversation moves from music fandom to plea terms, judge behavior, witness credibility, or trial legitimacy.

\section{Event-by-Event Interpretive Findings}
This section translates the quantitative outputs into a narrative that reads like a report, not a notebook. The goal is to answer: what changed, when did it change, and what is the most defensible interpretation given confounding and platform differences?

\subsection{May 2022 Indictment and Arrest}
The indictment/arrest window is the baseline shock. It re-anchors the discourse from music-first conversation to a hybrid legal-media conversation with persistent RICO vocabulary. The large post-window volume increase is the expected result of a major public event. This window is not a clean test of ThuggerDaily influence because mainstream coverage and social reaction are both maximal.

\subsection{December 2022 Gunna Plea}
Plea-related moments tend to polarize fan narratives (loyalty, betrayal, strategic necessity) and create long-tail conversation beyond the immediate news cycle. The event-window results show a large attention shift, while topic and platform composition help diagnose whether the post-window conversation is primarily procedural/legal or identity/fandom-driven.

\subsection{November 2023 Lyrics as Evidence}
The lyrics ruling is a high-salience framing event: it connects the trial to broader debates about art, criminalization, and evidentiary standards. Even if sentiment does not move dramatically in aggregate, topic shifts can show a rise in legal-process language and media framing terms.

\subsection{Opening Statements (Late 2023)}
Opening statements are a classic narrative inflection point. They generate quotable claims, interpretable ``stories'' about what the trial is about, and rapid recirculation through clips and summaries. In this window, ThuggerDaily is most plausibly acting as a transmission and framing node rather than a primary driver of attention.

\subsection{June--July 2024 Recusal Crisis}
The recusal period is a procedural legitimacy shock. Public discourse can become simultaneously more supportive of the defendant while more negative toward the process. This is exactly where topic analysis matters: it distinguishes ``support'' language from ``procedural fairness'' language.

\subsection{October 2024 Plea and Release}
The October 31, 2024 plea/release is the most important resolution moment for the Young Thug narrative. The defensible claim is not that social media created the outcome, but that accounts like ThuggerDaily can accelerate and shape the public-facing interpretation of the outcome in the immediate post-event window.

\subsection{December 2024 Trial End}
The end of proceedings for remaining defendants shifts discourse from live uncertainty to retrospective closure. In this phase, amplification accounts function more like memory and framing channels: what is remembered, what is emphasized, and how reputational narratives settle.

\section{Cross-Platform Narrative Mechanics}
\subsection{YouTube as High-Volume Reaction Archive}
YouTube dominates the record count because courtroom streams, legal commentary videos, and comment sections produce dense records. That makes YouTube excellent for measuring volume and breadth of reaction, but it also means raw counts overweight comment behavior relative to institutional reporting.

\subsection{X/Twitter as Fast Narrative Transmission}
X/Twitter is the exposure platform for ThuggerDaily. Its analytical value is speed, timestamp precision, and frame transmission. A single post can compress courtroom information into a shareable claim, which then diffuses into other spaces.

\subsection{Institutional Media as Framing Inputs}
Newspapers, local news, and magazines have fewer records but high framing value. Their language often becomes the vocabulary that social platforms quote, argue with, or remix.

\subsection{Music Signals as Competing Attention Shocks}
Spotify and Billboard are not comment platforms, but they capture attention shifts tied to releases and chart movement. Those signals matter because they change the mix of public conversation from legal procedure toward career narrative and identity.

Empirically, this creates a familiar interpretive problem in event windows: a trial hearing can increase legal-topic volume at the same time that a release, feature, chart movement, or music-media cycle increases music-topic volume. In the immediate aftermath of high-salience legal moments, the public narrative can therefore \emph{recompose} rather than move in a single direction. One portion of the ecosystem reacts to courtroom information (motions, plea terms, judge behavior, legitimacy), while another portion pivots toward career trajectory (``what happens to the music?'', ``what does this mean for the artist?'', ``who is loyal?''). This is why the report treats music sources as \textbf{competing attention shocks} and why topic shifts are read as mixture changes: legal-topic increases can coexist with a short-run rise in music attention that partially dilutes purely legal framing even when total volume spikes.

\section{Platform Interpretation}
The platform composition affects every result. YouTube dominates raw records because video comments and video metadata are dense. X/Twitter is central to the ThuggerDaily exposure mechanism but is not the largest total record source. Magazines and newspapers provide fewer records but often contain higher-density narrative framing. Spotify and Billboard are not public-opinion platforms, but they provide music-attention signals around album releases and chart movement.

This multi-platform structure is a strength because it prevents the report from equating one platform with the public. It is also a limitation because measurement units differ. A YouTube comment, a tweet, a magazine article, and a Billboard chart row are not identical observations. The dashboard's role is to make these differences visible while still allowing an integrated public narrative view.

\section{What Can Be Said About ``Affecting the Trial''}
The phrase ``affected the trial'' needs a disciplined interpretation. Based on this dataset, the correct claim is not that ThuggerDaily altered a judge's ruling, changed a plea negotiation, or caused legal outcomes. The defensible claim is that ThuggerDaily appears to have affected the \emph{public narrative environment around the trial} by posting near key legal events and potentially amplifying selected frames.

This report should not be used to claim that ThuggerDaily affected judicial decision-making, plea negotiations, witness behavior, or legal outcomes. The evidence supports a narrower public-discourse claim: ThuggerDaily was a visible amplification channel whose activity often coincided with measurable changes in public attention, sentiment, engagement, and topic composition around key legal events.

Three mechanisms are plausible:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Attention amplification:} posts near legal events can increase the number of people who see or discuss the event.
\item \textbf{Frame selection:} the account can choose which aspects of courtroom activity become salient to followers.
\item \textbf{Sentiment coordination:} repeated support language can strengthen or concentrate fan sentiment around an entity.
\end{enumerate}

These mechanisms are media effects, not legal-causal proof. The report therefore uses the language of temporal association, influence signal, event-linked discourse shift, and public narrative response.

\section{Key Findings and Claims}
These findings are stated at a level of certainty supported by the data and observational design:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item The YSL trial period (2022--2024) contains repeated event-linked shifts in public discourse volume, engagement, and topic mix.
\item ThuggerDaily is a measurable exposure stream (773 posts in the processed ThuggerDaily dataset) that is frequently active in the same short windows where public attention moves.
\item The strongest evidence of association appears as short-run, event-conditioned movement (same-day to 7-day windows), not as stable long-run predictive power at daily resolution.
\item Topic shifts provide the clearest interpretation layer: during resolution moments (e.g., the October 2024 plea/release), the conversation partially rebalances from procedural/legal language toward music identity and fandom framing.
\item None of the analyses identify randomized causal effects or legal causation; the correct language is temporal association and public narrative influence signal, not legal-causal responsibility.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Operational Implications}
In a legal-media intelligence workflow, the results support a practical monitoring posture:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Treat the event calendar as an attention calendar:} courtroom moments and major media stories reliably create short-run volume and engagement spikes.
\item \textbf{Monitor amplification nodes:} high-visibility accounts can concentrate attention and frames during post-event windows; their activity is useful as an early indicator of narrative direction.
\item \textbf{Separate ``tone'' from ``topic'':} sentiment can stay flat while topic mix shifts meaningfully from music talk to legal process talk (or vice versa).
\item \textbf{Keep platform differences explicit:} YouTube is a high-volume reaction archive; X/Twitter is a fast transmission layer; institutional sources are low-volume but high framing value.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Overall Implications and Interpretation}
Taken together, the results support a practical interpretation that is stronger than ``the account posted a lot'' but weaker than ``the account caused outcomes.'' The YSL trial is a long-running, high-salience public event. That means attention is repeatedly reallocated: major hearings, rulings, and plea moments create sharp bursts of public conversation; music releases and artist news create competing bursts that pull conversation back toward career identity and fandom.

Within that environment, ThuggerDaily is best understood as a \textbf{narrative transmission channel}. It compresses complex trial information into fast, legible frames, and it reliably participates in post-event windows when attention is already high. This matters because attention alone is not the story: the public also needs a \emph{story about what the event means}. In those windows, influential accounts can shape interpretation by selecting clips, emphasizing procedural fairness or unfairness, and amplifying support-oriented language.

The data suggest three operational takeaways. First, \textbf{event timing dominates}: courtroom shocks and major media coverage are the primary drivers of spikes in public volume and engagement. Second, \textbf{influence is episodic}: averaged over multiple years, a single account will rarely look like a strong global predictor; influence shows up when you zoom into the windows that matter. Third, \textbf{topic shift is the clearest interpretive signal}: resolution moments can move the conversation away from procedural language and toward music, identity, and fandom framing, even if sentiment changes are modest.

In a professional setting, the right use of this report is to support disciplined situational awareness. It does not tell a legal team what will happen in court. It does tell analysts what the public is paying attention to, how that attention is framed, and when a visible amplification channel is aligned with broader narrative movement. That is the actionable layer: public legitimacy, reputational dynamics, and narrative momentum are not courtroom facts, but they are measurable features of high-profile legal events and they can be monitored with the same discipline applied to other operational risk signals.

\section{Limitations}
\subsection{Observational Design}
The analysis is observational. There is no randomized assignment of ThuggerDaily exposure. People who follow or react to ThuggerDaily are not randomly selected from the public. Trial events and posts may be jointly caused by the same news.

\subsection{Omitted Confounders}
The model does not fully observe all mainstream coverage, private group chats, TikTok, full X/Twitter firehose activity, courtroom livestream reach, or recommendation algorithm exposure. Omitted confounders can explain part of any observed association.

\subsection{Platform Bias}
The corpus is not platform-balanced. YouTube records dominate the sample. X/Twitter is central to the exposure but not the majority of downstream records. Platform-specific interpretation remains necessary.

\subsection{Sentiment Error}
Sentiment models can misread slang, sarcasm, legal terms, hip-hop discourse, and fan-coded language. Topic labels are useful abstractions but not perfect semantic truth.

\subsection{Engagement Denominators}
Engagement-rate denominators are inconsistent across sources. The dashboard uses analytics, impressions, or views where available and falls back to raw engagement where denominators are missing. This makes cross-platform engagement comparisons approximate.

\section{Conclusion}
The NarrativePulse analysis supports a nuanced conclusion: ThuggerDaily was a measurable public-narrative exposure stream during the YSL RICO trial, but the evidence does not support claims of legal causation. Public discourse shifted in volume, sentiment, engagement, and topic composition around major trial events, and ThuggerDaily activity frequently appeared in the same short-run windows. The strongest signal appears around high-salience legal moments, especially the October 31, 2024 plea/release window, where public attention rose sharply and ThuggerDaily showed substantial post-event activity and engagement.

The appropriate interpretation is therefore not that ThuggerDaily caused trial outcomes, but that it likely contributed to the public narrative environment by amplifying attention, selecting frames, and concentrating fan/support discourse. For legal-media intelligence, this is a meaningful result: public legitimacy, reputational dynamics, and narrative momentum are not courtroom facts, but they are measurable features of high-profile legal events.
